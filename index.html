Design Forward · Client Design · FY27

Design at the front of the process, not behind it

Design Forward is Client Design's FY27 initiative for getting to the front of the process — tracking work more visibly, earning strategic influence earlier, and using AI to reduce overhead, not add it.

Prepared by Mirella, Senior Manager · Client Design
Audience Ava DeVoe, Ashley Adams
Status Draft for input
Updated —
Context

What I've been observing

Four weeks into leading Client Design, a few patterns have come into focus through 1:1s with the team, xf partner conversations, and direct observation of how work moves through the process. These patterns are connected — and with FY27 starting in July, now is the right moment to address them together rather than as separate problems.

"Design should be at the front of the process, not behind it. That means requirements clarity earlier, design work visible enough to be trusted as a strategic partner, and prototyping fast enough to lead — not react."

Leadership has already stated what good looks like: Ava and Ashley don't want design running like engineering. Gray wants design owning early prototyping for strategic buy-in — not PM. The company expects AI in the workflow, though standards haven't been formalized yet. This initiative is Client Design's concrete response to all three of those signals.

Why FY27 is the right moment

Teams are resetting priorities. The triad model is new and norms are still forming — there's more openness to establishing shared practices now than mid-cycle. The SVP-level interest in AI gives design air cover to propose changes that would otherwise require more bottom-up persuasion.

Problem framing

Three connected problems — one initiative

These aren't separate issues. They compound each other, and solving any one in isolation doesn't move the needle enough.

Requirements gap

PM ticket quality is inconsistent. Some designers have been filling the void — acting as de facto PMs to get work started. That's not what Ava wants, and it's not what design should be doing.

Visibility gap

Design work isn't tracked in a way xf partners can see. But leadership doesn't want design running like engineering. The fix is lightness — not Jira adoption as-is.

Influence gap

Gray wants design leading strategy at project start — owning early prototyping for buy-in. That's only possible if design can move fast enough to shape the conversation before PM does.

A designer who gets clear requirements earlier, has their work visible on shared boards, and can generate AI-assisted explorations quickly is a designer who leads strategy — not one who reacts to it. The solution addresses all three problems with a single coherent workflow.

Solution design principles

What any solution must satisfy

These constraints are non-negotiable. They reflect what leadership has said, what designers actually need, and what will realistically stick.

✓
Designers stay in their natural workflow
Figma is where design work happens. Any visibility solution must produce Jira artifacts as a byproduct of work already being done — not require a separate Jira maintenance habit on top of everything else.
✓
Visible enough — not perfectly tracked
The goal isn't engineering-grade sprint tracking. It's enough visibility that a PM or eng lead can answer "where is design on this?" without asking. A current phase label and a Figma link is often sufficient.
✓
Designers can move first
Any solution must support — not slow down — design's ability to generate early explorations and lead strategy at project start. If it adds overhead to the front of the process, it defeats the purpose.
✓
Works with what already exists
Jira, Figma, Slack, and Atlassian integrations are already in use. The solution extends these — no new tool requiring adoption from both design and eng.
✗
Designers do not own requirements
AI can help translate or gap-detect, but PM accountability for requirements stays with PM. No solution should quietly automate designers into a PM role.
✗
No solution requiring xf behavior change to function at launch
The design-owned pieces need to work independently first. xf behavior change follows once there's something demonstrable — it can't be a prerequisite for the first step.
!
AI use is expected but not yet formalized
Everyone is told to use AI but there's no org standard yet — Ava is working on that with other leaders. This initiative can be Client Design's early proof point for what thoughtful AI adoption looks like specifically in design.
Solution directions

Three directions — one workflow

These build on each other. Direction 1 is the foundation that makes Direction 3 possible. They're sequenced, not parallel — start with what design controls entirely, add xf complexity only after proof of concept.

1
AI-assisted requirements translation
Addresses the requirements gap · Design moves first · Recommended starting point
✦ Highest AI leverage

When a PM creates a Jira ticket or PRD — complete or not — Claude translates it into a design-ready brief: clear design questions, known unknowns flagged, scenarios to consider, and a first set of exploration directions. Designer reviews and approves in one step. PM stays accountable for requirements; designers stop being blocked by incomplete ones.

When requirements are genuinely missing, Claude generates a draft requirements brief from available context — project name, past tickets, Confluence pages — and routes it to the PM to confirm before design starts. Designers never write the requirements. They just can't be blocked waiting for them.

What designers do differently

Review a Claude-generated design brief instead of interpreting a raw Jira ticket. Approve or redirect in one step. Start exploration from a clearer foundation with less ambiguity.

What becomes visible to xf

The design brief — linked in Jira — signals design has started, what they're working from, and what's still open. Visibility as a byproduct, not additional reporting.

2
Lightweight design status layer in Jira
Addresses the visibility gap · Design-native format · AI-maintained
✦ AI reduces maintenance overhead

A minimal Jira ticket format for design work — not engineering-style stories with story points and acceptance criteria, but a design-specific template: current phase (Explore / Align / Build), Figma link, open questions, and last updated. AI watches where designers already communicate — Figma comments, Slack updates, design review notes — and drafts ticket updates automatically. Designer approves with one click.

The ticket is read-only for eng and PM — they see where design is without needing to interpret Figma. It doesn't replace design reviews. It removes the "is this ready?" ambiguity that currently causes either premature builds or passive reviews.

What designers do differently

Approve AI-drafted Jira updates rather than writing them. One-tap confirmation from Slack — not context-switching into Jira to author a ticket update from scratch.

What becomes visible to xf

Current design phase, Figma link, and open questions on the team board. Enough for PM and eng to know status without asking — not enough to be confused with a build signal.

3
AI-powered early exploration for strategic lead
Addresses the influence gap · Enables design-led strategy · Builds on Direction 1
✦ AI as speed multiplier

Using the design brief from Direction 1, Claude generates 2–3 intentionally divergent exploration approaches at low fidelity — rough enough not to be mistaken for final design, concrete enough for stakeholders to react to. Designer curates and directs. The goal is something in front of product leadership and eng before PM has fully shaped the direction. That's exactly what Gray wants.

Speed is the prerequisite for design-led strategy. If design can have explorations ready in hours rather than days, they own the first conversation. If it takes a week, PM fills the vacuum. AI compression of the exploration phase makes the strategic lead realistic, not aspirational.

Why multiple divergent approaches — not variations

The approaches should differ meaningfully in structure, flow, or technical implication — not just visual style. When eng sees three genuinely different directions, they're forced into an analyst role: which has the most dependency risk, which reuses existing patterns, which opens the most edge case exposure. That analytical engagement is exactly what passive design reviews fail to generate. The goal is to surface eng's judgment early, not validate a direction they haven't truly evaluated.

Deliberate low fidelity is a feature, not a limitation

Explore-tier artifacts should look nothing like a Figma handoff. If they don't look finished, they can't be mistaken for finished. Use rough wireframe fidelity, explicit annotation of open questions, and a visually distinct treatment from Figma deliverables. The fidelity gap between Explore and Build tier should be unmistakable — not just a label difference.

The framing that lands with eng leads

"We want your expertise earlier, not later — these artifacts are designed to surface your judgment, not bypass it." That's a fundamentally different ask than "review this design," and it tends to get more genuine engagement. Pair each exploration with 3–5 specific reaction questions so eng knows exactly what input is being asked for — dependency risk, pattern reuse, unknowns to spike on.

What designers do differently

Direct AI-generated explorations rather than originating from scratch. Spend creative energy on curation and refinement. Move fast enough to lead, not react to PM-shaped requirements.

What becomes visible to xf

Explore-tier artifacts — clearly labeled, not for build — surface as evidence design is engaged and leading. Strategic presence before PM has locked direction.

Design-led strategy

Minimum information design needs to take the lead

For design to lead at the front of a project — generating explorations that shape the strategic conversation rather than reacting to PM-defined requirements — there's a minimum viable information set. Not a perfect PRD. Not a fully spec'd ticket. Just enough to move with intent.

The core principle

Design doesn't need complete requirements to start exploring — it needs enough context to explore in the right direction. The exploration itself surfaces the questions that make requirements better. That's the strategic value design brings to the front of the process.

Information Why design needs it Priority Who owns it
The user problem or job to be done
What is the user trying to accomplish? What's currently broken or missing for them?		 Without this, explorations are decorating a solution rather than solving a problem. It's the single most important input. Must have PM
The business goal or success metric
What does the business need this to accomplish? How will we know it worked?		 Grounds exploration in outcomes, not features. Prevents design from optimizing for the wrong thing when there are multiple valid directions. Must have PM
Known constraints
Technical limits, platform scope, timeline pressure, non-negotiable product decisions already made.		 Shapes which directions are worth exploring. Saves design from investing in an approach that's technically or commercially impossible before review. Must have PM + Eng
Who the user is
Persona, segment, or a specific type of agent or consumer. Doesn't need to be a full persona doc — just enough to picture someone.		 Grounds exploration in a real person's context, not an abstract user. Critical for RealPro and agent-facing work where the audience is highly specific. Must have PM + Design
Existing patterns or related past work
Is there a prior design, a related feature, or a pattern in the system this should connect to?		 Prevents reinventing the wheel and helps explorations feel coherent with the existing product. Also flags where breaking from existing patterns is intentional vs. accidental. Should have Design

Is there a direction leadership is already leaning? A solution someone has already proposed internally? What should design not try to solve in this round? What related problems are being deferred?		 Prevents explorations from going too wide and generating stakeholder questions that slow momentum. Scope boundaries are as useful as scope definitions. Nice to have PM
On incomplete requirements

When the must-haves are missing, design shouldn't wait and shouldn't fill the gap by writing requirements. The right move — and what Direction 1 enables — is for AI to draft the missing questions and route them back to PM for confirmation before design starts. Design stays in its lane. The process doesn't stall.

What design brings to the table — even before requirements are final

Design doesn't need to wait for a complete picture to add value. With just the user problem and business goal in hand, design can:

Generate multiple problem framings

Different ways of framing the same problem often reveal which assumptions are baked in. This surfaces strategic questions before solution exploration begins.

Map the user journey gaps

Show where the current experience breaks down before proposing a solution. Creates a shared fact base for the xf conversation that PM can react to with constraints.

Surface edge cases and scenarios early

Articulate what the solution needs to handle beyond the happy path. These questions improve requirement quality before PM hands off a ticket.

Establish the design brief

A structured document capturing what design understood, what it assumed, and what it still needs. Makes the handoff legible and gives PM a concrete artifact to react to.

AI in the workflow

Where AI fits — and where it doesn't

AI is the tool that makes the workflow sustainable. Without it, what's being proposed here adds overhead. With it, visibility and early exploration become byproducts of work designers are already doing.

High-leverage uses

Requirements translation and gap detection

Claude reads an incoming Jira ticket or PRD and produces a structured design brief. When inputs are incomplete, it flags what's missing and drafts a requirements question back to PM — before design has started. Designers never write requirements. They just don't get blocked by incomplete ones.

Jira status drafting from existing activity

Claude watches Figma comments, Slack design channel updates, and design review notes — surfaces a draft Jira status update for the designer to approve in one click. Visibility as a byproduct, not a tax on designer time.

Divergent exploration generation

Given a design brief, Claude generates 2–3 rough exploration approaches — intentionally different in structure, flow, or emphasis — for the designer to curate and direct. Compresses blank-canvas time. Designer judgment shapes the output; AI removes the overhead of the first draft.

Scenario gap analysis

Before a design advances from Explore to Align tier, Claude reviews the proposed flow and surfaces edge cases and error states not yet accounted for. Makes incompleteness visible before Figma investment — reducing late-stage rework without adding a review gate.

Design spike story type

To make eng's early feasibility input legible within their quota system, propose a defined story category — a half-point or unpointed "Feasibility React" — that gives eng a trackable deliverable for reacting to Explore-tier artifacts. The deliverable is specific: review the exploration approaches, answer 3–5 structured questions on dependency risk and pattern reuse, flag unknowns. It shows up in the sprint, it counts, and it stops early eng engagement from being invisible overhead that competes with story completion targets.

Where AI does not help

Design decision-making

AI generates options and surfaces gaps. It does not choose direction, approve design, or signal readiness for build. The designer's judgment is the only thing that advances work. This distinction matters especially for designers cautious about AI and professional identity.

Replacing PM requirements ownership

AI translates and gap-detects — it cannot replace PM accountability for requirements. A solution that lets PMs off the hook by having AI fill gaps will worsen the PM quality problem over time, not solve it.

Building triad trust

The feasibility alignment problem is partly relational. New triads haven't built the psychological safety for honest early reactions. No AI workflow fixes that. Process creates the structure; trust is built person to person.

Connection to Ava's AI formalization effort

Ava is working with other leaders to formalize AI use across the org. This initiative is Client Design's specific contribution — a tested set of AI use cases in a design workflow, with real pilot data on what reduced overhead looks like. That's a more valuable input to formalization than a theoretical framework.

Alignment needed

What we need to move forward

Most of this initiative is design-owned and can start immediately. A few things require explicit alignment before moving cross-functionally.

From Ava — ownership and scope

How do you want to structure ownership? You leading with me executing, or me leading with you as sponsor? What's the expected output and timeline from the SVP's perspective? Is this scoped to Client Design or intended to scale? Are there existing efforts I should connect with rather than run in parallel?

From Ava + Ashley — the design-native Jira template

Sign-off on the lightest possible ticket format before it goes to eng and PM leads. This is the artifact that makes visibility real without engineering-style overhead. Needs to be agreed before FY27 kickoff so it doesn't launch into ambiguity.

From Ashley — eng lead alignment on the feasibility story type

The sprint ritual in Direction 3 requires eng leads to agree that early feasibility input is worth a half-point story. The framing: late-stage design rework is a sprint risk that affects eng delivery too. This is a shared investment in sprint predictability, not design adding to their backlog.

From PM leads — alignment on the artifact stage gate

A Figma file not marked Build-tier should not be a build signal. That convention needs PM lead buy-in to stick. Whitney and the PM leads need to be sponsors of this norm, not just observers. This conversation is best initiated after the design-side foundation is already running.

On timing

The first two asks are prerequisites before anything launches. The last two can follow once the pilot is running and there's something concrete to show. Asking for xf behavior change before design has demonstrated the value is the wrong sequence.
